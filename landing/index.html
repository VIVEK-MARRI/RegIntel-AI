§ 01 The problem, stated precisely

Compliance teams don't drown in information. They drown in cross-references.

A new circular amends a master direction that superseded an earlier notification. The answer you give the business team depends on which clause is currently in force — and finding out means reading six documents end-to-end. Again.

PAIN · 01

Supersession chains you trace by hand.

A 2024 circular amends a 2021 master direction that itself superseded a 2018 notification. The clause you need might live in any of the three — and you only find out which by reading each in full and checking the footnotes. There is no index. There is no map.

PAIN · 02

Verification that means re-reading the source.

Before a generated answer goes into an audit response, a board note, or a regulator-facing filing, someone has to confirm it actually says what the system claims it says. That's the whole job, done manually, on every answer, every time. The cost isn't the lookup — it's the doubt.

PAIN · 03

No map of what affects what.

A change to one master direction ripples through every circular that referenced it, every notification it amended, every institution it governs. Without a knowledge graph, you find out about the ripple when something breaks — usually during an inspection.

§ 02 How retrieval actually works

Two kinds of search, fused, then re-read for precision.

A compliance question isn't a keyword query. It's a question about meaning, section numbers, defined terms, and the exact way a clause is worded. RegIntel-AI runs both retrieval paths in parallel, fuses them, and then a cross-encoder reads the top candidates against the question itself.

Retrieval pipeline Stage 01 / 06
01

The query, as asked.

A compliance officer types: "What is the current minimum CAR for deposit-taking NBFCs?" No special syntax, no boolean operators, no field tags. The question is encoded and routed to two retrieval paths in parallel.

02

Dense semantic retrieval (BGE embeddings).

The system matches the question against every passage by meaning. "Capital adequacy ratio" matches "CAR." "Minimum regulatory capital" matches "fifteen per cent of risk-weighted assets." This catches paraphrase — the way two clauses say the same thing in different words.

03

BM25 keyword retrieval, in parallel.

A keyword pass catches exact terms — section numbers like 4.2(b), defined terms like "deposit-taking NBFC," circular IDs like RBI/2024-XX/012 — that semantic search can smooth over. Some questions hinge on a specific number. BM25 doesn't miss those.

04

Reciprocal Rank Fusion.

The two ranked lists are merged. A passage that ranks high in both moves to the top. A passage that ranks high in only one is weighted accordingly. This isn't a vote — it's a weighted combination that respects how strongly each retriever committed to its ranking.

05

Cross-encoder re-ranking.

The top candidates are read pairwise against the question — not by similarity, but by a model trained to ask: does this passage actually answer this question? The cross-encoder is slower than retrieval, so it only runs on the shortlist. What it produces is a re-ranked list where position reflects actual answerability, not just topical overlap.

06

Cited result, with source attached.

What reaches the generation step is the passage that survived every stage — with its document, page, section, and exact text attached. The answer is then generated against this passage, not against the model's parametric memory. If the passage doesn't support a claim, the guard withholds it.

§ 03 Citation & hallucination guarding

If the text doesn't say it, the system doesn't say it.

Every generated sentence is checked against the passages it claims to cite. If a claim cannot be traced to specific text in a retrieved source, it is withheld — not softened, not hedged, not paraphrased into something defensible. Withheld.

§ 04 Regulatory knowledge graph

A new circular doesn't arrive alone.

Every circular, master direction, and amendment is mapped to the entities it touches: the institutions it governs, the documents it amends or supersedes, the topics it addresses. When a new circular lands, RegIntel-AI knows what it changes — before you ask.

Amends
A new circular modifies clauses in an existing master direction. The relationship is directional and dated.
Supersedes
A new circular replaces an earlier one in full or in part. The earlier document remains in the graph, marked as superseded.
Applies to
A direction or circular governs a specific class of institution — deposit-taking NBFCs, insurers, banks, fintechs.
Governs
A document addresses a regulatory topic — asset classification, capital adequacy, KYC, reporting.
